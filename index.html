About

Creative Alchemy creates rolls of paperless towels that have a variety of uses in the household. See some examples below.

Replacement for paper towels in the kitchen

Napkins - keep a roll on a free standing holder on the table

In the car - keep some in an empty tissue box to use for cleaning up spills or wiping off foggy windows

In the bathroom - For guests to use to dry their hands instead of the same hand towel the family uses.

Camping -since they wash up and dry quickly use them to wash/dry camping dishes and then hang on a line to dry and reuse.

Baby wipes - keep a roll by the changing table along with a bottle of diluted baby shampoo. Dampen the towel with the diluted shampoo and wipe up baby bottoms.

Kitchen - dampen one towel, ring out and place beneath cutting board to keep it from sliding around on the counter top

Inspiration

I am a creative person by nature and am always looking for an opportunity to make things with my hands. I also value practicality and functionality and so find purely artistic creative endeavors somewhat frivolous and unsatisfying. Over the years I have made and sold goofy polar fleece hats (fun, but highly seasonally dependent when it comes to selling them), soaps and lotions (again fun, but at such a low price point you need to sell a lot just to earn back the fee for setting up at a craft show, never mind making any profit above that), and needle felted dolls (so time consuming that I felt guilty charging an amount that seemed like a reasonable return on something so thoroughly non-functional, plus I fell in love with them and didn't want to part with any). I was looking for a creative outlet that would pay for itself, plus some profit, that satisfied my inner drive toward environmental stewardship/practicality/creative expression. The Paperless towels seem to satisfy all these desires.

Story

The idea for Paperless Towels began in 2013 when I began to look for an alternative to using hand towels instead of paper towels around the kitchen. I had moved away from paper towels several years before but found that although I was using less paper, I was doing more laundry and therefore using more water and energy. Plus all our hand towels were full of stains. I found a product available on ETSY that looked like a roll of paper towels but was made with terry cloth. I liked the general idea, but those towels were held together using snaps. And because the terry cloth is thick, there were only 12 or so towels on a roll for something like $40. I put my thinking cap on. I didn't like the snaps - too fussy, both in the production and the use of the towels. I eventually hit on the idea of flannel since it sticks to itself. Then, I took the concept one step further and thought "What about recycling used flannel sheets?" This adaptation makes the product that much more appealing to me: I'm not promoting the production of new fabric, with all the energy use and pollution that comes with that, plus the material itself is less costly than brand new fabric. I proto-typed the towels at home for 2 years, first trying to just cut the edges with a zig-zag scissor thinking that would reduce the fraying - it didn't. We were picking threads of flannel fabric off of all our clothing! I then tried to finish the edges with my sewing machine - the looked fine at first but didn't hold up well to several washings. I finally invested in a Serger and was able to finish the edges in a way that holds up well to multiple washings. I also played with various size options until I hit on a size that fits well on a standard paper towel roll and that I can cut reasonable efficiently from various sizes of sheets.
